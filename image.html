The Chinese Opium Smoker No. 12

By Arshan Hejazi, for class HI367

Analysis of the Image

The image titled The Chinese Opium Smoker No. 12 comes from a series of illustrations showing the destructive effects of the opium trade in China. It depicts an opium smoker struggling to survive in the winter, eventually finding shelter in a cave where he dies. This visual representation directly contrasts with the British perspectives found in the Records of the Royal Commission on Opium. In the document, British officials repeatedly defend the opium trade by focusing on its economic importance to the Indian government. They describe opium as a necessary source of revenue and downplay its harmful effects by shifting responsibility to Chinese authorities.

The image challenges that narrative by focusing on the personal and social consequences of addiction. While the Commission frames the trade in terms of legality, treaties, and financial stability, the illustration forces viewers to confront the human suffering caused by those policies. Together, the document and the image reveal the gap between imperial justification and lived reality, helping us better understand the moral complexity of the opium trade.
