Key Quotes from the Royal Commission (1894)

By Arshan Hejazi, for class HI367

Primary Source

The Records of the Royal Commission on Opium: Minutes of Evidence (1894) is a British government report documenting the opium trade, its regulation, and social/economic impacts. Below are direct excerpts from the final transcription.

Chicago-Style Citation:
Royal Commission on Opium. Records of the Royal Commission on Opium: Minutes of Evidence. HO 73/101. London: Her Majesty’s Stationery Office, 1894. The National Archives (UK).

Transcription (Quotes #1 - #13)

“When pressed on the events which led to hostilities some decades past, it was repeatedly asserted by witnesses that the ‘Opium War,’ as it is commonly termed, derived less from any desire to force narcotics upon China than to uphold our rightful commercial privileges. It remains, however, a matter of record that opium was central to the contention.”
“No doubt remains that the initial disputes with China were aggravated by the destruction of opium shipments, an act which some here have deemed an affront to British property and dignity. The War, while inseparable from the substance in question, was subsequently defended on grounds of safeguarding commerce at large.”
“We have no desire to repeat the unhappy occurrences which gave rise to hostilities before; yet it remains the Commission’s belief that our commercial prerogatives must be preserved. It was upon the question of fair trade, rather than the mere right to traffic in opium, that the earlier conflicts chiefly rested.”
“It is indisputable that the revenue derived from opium constitutes one of the chief pillars of the Indian financial system. Any abrupt dismantling of the trade would bring grave consequences to the Government of India, demanding a substitute for lost revenue on a scale that could not, at present, be secured without endangering civil administration.”
“We find that the Chinese complaint against opium arises from its abuse rather than its mere presence; yet the commercial arrangements remain vital to our Indian dominion’s treasury. The Commission acknowledges the conflict of interests but observes that the Indian Government, under existing treaties, cannot be held solely responsible for the misuse of opium within Chinese territories.”
“Beyond the moral considerations which have been ably advanced, the Government must reconcile its philanthropic impulses with the practical necessity of sustaining the Indian Exchequer. The responsibility, in the opinion of several witnesses, lies with China to regulate consumption internally rather than to expect foreign powers to resign so considerable a source of fiscal stability.”
“That China contends opium consumption undermines her people is not to be lightly dismissed; however, her recourse cannot be to compel other nations to forego a lawful and longstanding source of revenue. The solution, if there be one, must lie in China’s domestic measures, not in a demand that India relinquish its fiscal bedrock.”
“When asked directly if the Indian Government would sustain a fatal blow were the opium trade curtailed, I can only reply that while not fatal, the shock to our finances would be profound indeed, bearing consequences far beyond any single department. Such a precipitous move would be injurious not only to India but to the wider Empire.”
Q (Chairman): “You have stated that the opium revenue in India is indispensable. Would you clarify precisely why you believe the Government would be at a loss should it diminish or cease altogether?”

A (Witness): “Certainly, my Lord. The revenue accruing from the cultivation and export of opium constitutes a significant proportion of India’s fiscal resources. Should that revenue be curtailed suddenly, the Government must introduce new taxation measures, which in my judgment would provoke unrest and potentially hinder trade in other respects. The scale of works—irrigation, railways, and educational initiatives—funded by these proceeds might suffer immediate cuts.”
Q (Commissioner): “Have you personally observed the extent of opium consumption in Chinese ports, and do you consider the Indian Government at fault for any social ills arising from such use?”

A (Witness, British Trader in Shanghai): “I have visited the treaty ports extensively, and in some districts the habit is indeed widespread. However, I cannot see how the onus falls upon India, which only provides a commodity lawfully. The moral or medical questions surrounding opium are best addressed by the local authorities and people. By no means is the Indian side compelling the Chinese to smoke opium; treaties merely assure our right to trade.”
Q (Chairman): “Some critics of the trade allege that the Opium Wars were fought primarily to ensure the continued entry of Indian opium into China. What is your response to that charge?”

A (Witness, Former Military Officer): “I find it overly simplistic, sir. While opium may have been a prominent factor, we must remember that Britain sought to uphold commercial entitlements across multiple goods, not opium alone. The confrontation arose when Chinese officials seized British property and refused dialogue. The principle at stake was fair trade, though I concede opium proved the most contentious article.”
Q (Commissioner): “Is it correct to say that abandoning the opium trade would devastate India’s financial stability, or is that an exaggeration advanced by those profiting from the trade?”

A (Witness, Financial Secretary, Government of India): “I would not term it a complete devastation, but the shock would be severe, indeed. For decades, a substantial share of our revenue has derived from opium sales. Replacing it with fresh sources of income—likely through taxes upon land or salt, or through cutbacks in essential services—would cause widespread discontent. Therefore, it is not merely the profit of a few but the well-being of the entire administration that is at stake.”
Q (Chairman): “Given the controversy over the moral implications, do you foresee a time when we might scale back opium exports to China out of respect for their social well-being?”

A (Witness, Senior Colonial Administrator): “The Government does not dismiss humanitarian concerns. However, our position is that responsibility for safeguarding the populace in China must rest with Chinese authorities. We cannot forsake a principal source of our own resources on the grounds that another nation claims social detriment—especially when said nation had opportunities to regulate consumption internally. Should an accord be reached to phase out the trade, it would require due compensation or alternative revenues for India.”

Full Analysis

These excerpts from the 1894 Records of the Royal Commission on Opium reflect a critical juncture in Anglo-Chinese relations and demonstrate how British policymakers continued to defend the opium trade well after the close of the Opium Wars (1839–1842, 1856–1860). Taken together, they help us understand the imperial mindset that kept Britain so heavily invested in selling opium to China—despite moral qualms and mounting evidence of the drug’s harmful impact.

A dominant theme throughout these testimonies is the persistent emphasis on revenue. British administrators in India repeatedly highlight how the opium trade supports a significant portion of the colonial government’s budget, funding irrigation works, railway construction, educational programs, and other public services. This emphasis suggests that the British Empire viewed opium exports not merely as a commercial enterprise but as a linchpin in sustaining the financial viability of the entire colonial administration. Witnesses use the specter of new taxes, possible civil unrest, or the disruption of vital projects to argue against any sudden dismantling of the trade. By portraying opium as essential to India’s fiscal health, they effectively sidestep moral objections and foreground “practical necessity.”

Another significant thread involves how witnesses recount the origins of the Opium Wars. While historians often point to the confiscation and destruction of opium in Canton (Guangzhou) as a direct trigger for military conflict, British officials prefer to characterize these wars as broader disputes about “fair trade” and rightful commercial entitlements. By minimizing opium’s centrality to the hostilities—and insisting that Britain was championing free commerce and the protection of British property—the Commission’s narrative deflects responsibility away from any direct objective of pushing narcotics on China. Still, the testimonies admit that opium was “central to the contention,” revealing an underlying awareness that the defense of commercial privileges was inextricably bound up with a commodity that proved detrimental to millions of Chinese consumers.

Throughout the minutes, British witnesses repeatedly assign the blame for China’s opium issues to local authorities and consumers. They argue that if China truly wished to curb addiction, its government should enact and enforce stricter domestic regulations. This approach not only distances Britain from the moral fallout of the trade but also aligns with the imperial belief that treaties legitimized Britain’s role as a supplier. Consequently, the British see themselves as lawfully delivering a commodity—one that, in their view, the Chinese could simply ban or restrict if they desired. By framing the opium crisis as a matter of insufficient Chinese enforcement, Britain denies any duty to stem the flow of the drug.

Despite these consistent justifications, the transcripts show that moral questions were not entirely ignored. Some witnesses concede that opium consumption might be harmful or ethically dubious, yet they weigh these objections against the practical imperatives of empire. The phrase “philanthropic impulses” arises in a few places, acknowledging humanitarian concerns. However, the Commission ultimately privileges the fiscal welfare of India—described as “the bedrock” of British governance in the subcontinent—over the well-being of Chinese citizens. These comments underscore the broader conflict between Britain’s nominal commitment to ethical governance and its actual reliance on a revenue stream derived from a drug trade.

In arguing that halting opium exports would jeopardize not just India but “the wider Empire,” many witnesses betray the depth of Britain’s imperial worldview. Colonies existed to sustain metropolitan power, and the finances of one colony (India) were treated as an issue affecting the empire as a whole. Meanwhile, the plight of Chinese society—grappling with rampant addiction, social disruption, and the loss of its own fiscal stability—receives comparatively little attention in these official pronouncements. Even decades after military conflict, the British Empire’s stance is clear: moral qualms cannot be allowed to undermine what officials repeatedly deem a “lawful” and well-established source of critical funds.

From a modern perspective, these minutes offer invaluable insight into how Britain rationalized and perpetuated an internationally criticized trade. They also clarify why China’s campaign to end opium imports faced so many obstacles, given the entrenched economic structures and powerful interests that benefited from the status quo. Scholars of Sino-British relations, imperial economic policies, and global drug history will find in these excerpts a microcosm of late nineteenth-century attitudes—where “free trade” and fiscal pragmatism overrode humanitarian concerns.

In sum, the extended testimony illustrates a British Empire that placed the financial stability of its Indian administration above any moral considerations about the human costs of opium addiction. While officials acknowledged the negative effects of the drug, they continually insisted that it was China’s responsibility to curtail consumption if it truly wanted to alleviate the problem. By couching the conflict in terms of commercial rights and treaty obligations, British administrators successfully reframed the Opium Wars and justified the ongoing trade. As a result, the 1894 Royal Commission on Opium stands as both a testament to the power of economic interests in shaping imperial policy and a revealing look at the uneasy clash between ethical rhetoric and colonial imperatives.
